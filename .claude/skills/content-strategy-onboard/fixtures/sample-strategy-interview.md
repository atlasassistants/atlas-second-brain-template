# Sample Strategy Interview — Priya Anand

**Exec:** Priya Anand, founder of Clearpath Coaching — an executive coaching firm working with mid-career leaders at professional services firms (consulting, accounting, law).

**EA:** Divya (running the strategy intake on Priya's behalf)

**Context:** `/voice-onboard` has already run. Voice.md is populated. Two audience pages exist: `consulting-partners` (primary — consulting firm partners seeking promotion or transition to firm leadership) and `midcareer-managers` (primary — mid-career managers at professional services firms deciding whether to push for partner track or exit).

---

## Phase 1: Domain definition

**Divya:** Priya, let's start with the big question. What are the 2-4 areas you want to be known for — where you have the most lived experience, the strongest opinions, and the most receipts?

**Priya:** Three areas, I think. The first is the partner track itself — how it actually works, how firms decide who makes it, and what people misunderstand about the criteria. I've now coached about 80 people through this and I've seen the same mistakes repeated constantly. I have very specific opinions about what people get wrong.

The second is what I'd call leadership identity for professionals. There's a whole category of people who've been trained their entire careers to be technically excellent and analytically rigorous, and then suddenly they're being evaluated on presence, on how they show up in a room, on their ability to sponsor others — and nobody told them the rules changed. That's a distinct problem from partner track. It's about renegotiating your identity as a leader.

The third is the exit question — when does staying on partner track stop making sense, and how do you make that call? I see a lot of clients who are two years away from partner and genuinely unsure if they want it. That's its own conversation. Commercially it's a different offer too — clients in that situation often end up in career transition coaching, not firm-leadership coaching.

**Divya:** Those three feel distinct. Let me test the scoping. The first is about the mechanics of the firm decision-making process — the path itself. The second is about the psychological and identity shift required to lead differently. The third is about the decision to stay or go. Does that capture it?

**Priya:** Yes, exactly. The first is structural — how the system works. The second is personal — who you need to become. The third is strategic — is this the right game for you.

---

## Phase 2: Pillar definition

**Divya:** Within Partner Track Mechanics — what are the 3 angles you return to most?

**Priya:** Sponsorship. Nobody explains how sponsorship actually works at professional services firms — it's not mentorship, it's not networking, it's a specific kind of advocacy that requires a partner to put their reputation behind you. Most candidates don't understand that distinction until it's too late.

Then promotion criteria transparency — or lack of it. Firms have explicit criteria on paper and implicit criteria in practice. I write a lot about the gap between the two and how to get the real criteria in front of you.

And intake timing — when in the cycle to make your case, who to be visible with, and how the deliberation processes actually work. This is very insider-knowledge territory. I get a lot of clients who had perfect portfolios and made their case at the wrong moment.

**Divya:** For Leadership Identity — three angles?

**Priya:** Presence under pressure — what it actually means to have executive presence in high-stakes moments (pitches, board presentations, difficult partner conversations). Not the soft fluffy version, the observable behavioral version.

Then delegation as identity shift — the moment when you realize that being technically right matters less than building others who can be technically right. That's a genuine identity crisis for a lot of high performers and I have very specific things to say about navigating it.

And cross-functional influence — how to lead people you have no authority over, which is the actual daily reality of a consulting partner. You're constantly influencing without authority.

**Divya:** For the Exit Question domain?

**Priya:** The stay-or-go framework itself — how to structure the decision so it's not purely emotional. I have a framework I use in coaching that I haven't fully written out publicly.

Then sunk cost recognition — the psychology of why people stay on a track they've already decided they don't want, and what it costs them. I have a lot of receipts here from clients who stayed 18 months too long.

And the reinvention risk map — if you leave, what are the real risks versus the perceived risks? I've seen people catastrophize and I've seen people underprepare. There's a specific set of variables that actually matter.

## Phase 3: Audience-domain mapping

**Divya:** Which audience is the primary reader for each domain?

**Priya:** Partner Track Mechanics — that's the consulting-partners audience. Almost entirely. The midcareer-managers audience is earlier in their journey; they're not yet close enough to the partner decision for this material to land.

Leadership Identity — this one serves both audiences. The consulting-partners audience is actively dealing with the identity shift right now. The midcareer-managers audience is earlier in the curve but they're starting to feel it too. Both are primary readers here.

Exit Question — this one is mostly midcareer-managers. The person who's genuinely asking "should I stay or go" is usually 3-5 years out from partner, not someone who's already made partner. Consulting-partners readers can find it useful but it's not written for them primarily.

**Divya:** Any pairing rules? When do you write companion pieces for both audiences vs. staying single-stream?

**Priya:** For Leadership Identity content, sometimes yes. If the topic is a specific leadership behavior that applies at both stages — say, managing up or executive presence in pitches — I'll write one piece aimed at the consulting-partners audience and a companion piece for managers framed at an earlier stage. But I don't pair by default. Only when the same underlying topic has genuinely clean angles for both.

For Partner Track Mechanics and Exit Question, I keep them single-stream. The audiences are different enough that a combined approach would dilute both.

## Phase 4: Commercial mapping

**Divya:** What are your active commercial offers?

**Priya:** Three. Clearpath 1:1 Coaching — that's my main offer, six months of individual coaching for the partner track or leadership identity work. Then Clearpath Exit Intensive — a 90-day program specifically for the stay-or-go decision. And I'm building a Partner Track Masterclass — a self-paced course for people who can't afford 1:1 coaching but want the system. That's 4-6 months out, still in development.

**Divya:** Commercial role per domain?

**Priya:** Partner Track Mechanics — this is direct conversion for both Clearpath 1:1 and the Partner Track Masterclass when it's live. People who read about how sponsorship actually works and think "I need someone to help me navigate this" — that's the CTA moment. Soft pointer to 1:1, then when the masterclass is live, direct CTA to that too.

Leadership Identity — this is primarily nurture. I'm not leading with a pitch in these pieces. The work is slower — someone who resonates with the leadership identity content will circle back when they're ready to commit to coaching. No direct CTA. Occasional soft pointer to the newsletter for more.

Exit Question — this is the most direct-conversion domain for the Exit Intensive. Someone reading about sunk cost recognition in the context of partner track is almost certainly in that situation. The CTA is clear: "If you're having this conversation with yourself, Clearpath Exit Intensive might be the right next step."

## Phase 5: Cadence

**Divya:** How many long-form pieces per year is sustainable for each domain?

**Priya:** Partner Track Mechanics — I could write 10 of these. There's enough material and it's what I get asked about constantly. That's the evergreen pillar.

Leadership Identity — maybe 8. These take more time to write because they're more nuanced. I can't crank these out on a schedule; I need to be in the right mode for them.

Exit Question — I'd say 6. This is a sensitive domain. I want to be deliberate about when I publish here, not write it on a timer. Some of my best Exit Question pieces have come out of specific client situations and I need the situation to exist before I can write about it.

**Divya:** Total of 24. You're on a bi-weekly newsletter cadence — that tracks. Does opportunistic-mode make sense for Exit Question?

**Priya:** Yes. I wouldn't commit to a fixed cadence for Exit Question. I'll publish when the material is strong, not to fill a slot.

## Phase 6: Distribution

**Divya:** What channels do you run?

**Priya:** Newsletter — The Clearpath Letter, bi-weekly. LinkedIn — I post actively. I don't run X. Email broadcasts for launches.

**Divya:** Distribution per domain?

**Priya:** Partner Track Mechanics — newsletter always, LinkedIn always. These are my highest-reach pieces. I'll repurpose every one of them into a LinkedIn long-form.

Leadership Identity — newsletter always, LinkedIn selectively. Not every leadership identity piece translates to LinkedIn — the format there rewards specificity and receipts, and some of my identity pieces are more reflective. I'll repurpose the ones with a strong concrete angle.

Exit Question — newsletter yes, LinkedIn no by default. This content is for people already in my orbit. I don't want Exit Question content circulating widely — it could attract the wrong clients or create the wrong brand signal. I'd rather keep it for newsletter subscribers who already know me.

## Phase 7: Voice frame

**Divya:** In Partner Track Mechanics — how does your voice feel different from your other domains?

**Priya:** More structural and a little harder-edged. I'm writing as someone who has watched these systems from the inside and has specific opinions about how they actually work versus how they're supposed to work. The moves I lean on most here are the receipt-first open — I'll often start with a specific client situation or a pattern I've seen repeated — and the skeptic counter, because the audience has heard a lot of generic career advice and I want to signal early that this is different.

**Divya:** What would you never write in Partner Track Mechanics?

**Priya:** Generic career advice with no professional-services-specific anchor. "Work on your personal brand" — fine elsewhere, useless here. And inspirational framing. These readers are not looking for motivation; they're looking for information. Don't write to inspire, write to inform.

**Divya:** Leadership Identity — voice flavor?

**Priya:** More personal, more exploratory. I'm giving myself more room to be uncertain here. The philosophical pullback is heavier. I'll often end a Leadership Identity piece without a clean resolution because the honest answer is that identity questions don't resolve neatly.

What I avoid here: the prescriptive "here are 5 steps to executive presence." That's the kind of piece that gets shared but doesn't actually help anyone. I'd rather write one honest piece about what presence actually requires than a listicle that performs insight without delivering it.

**Divya:** And Exit Question?

**Priya:** This is the most careful domain. I'm writing about something that costs people a lot — sometimes a decade of their career. The tone has to be steady, non-alarmist, and genuinely non-directive. I'm not telling them what to do. I'm helping them see their situation more clearly. I lean heavily on the builder-as-witness move here — "here's what I've seen clients experience" — rather than prescriptive framing.

What I never write in Exit Question: anything that romanticizes leaving. "Escaping corporate life" framing is completely off. And I never write anything that makes staying sound like failure. Both decisions are valid; my job is to help people make the right one for them.
