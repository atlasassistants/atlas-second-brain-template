# Sample Intake Transcript — Marcus Reyes

**Format:** Voice intake interview. EA (Jordan) with Marcus Reyes, founder of Torque Automation (workflow automation tooling for ops-heavy SMBs). 30-minute call.

---

**Jordan:** How does your team say you communicate? Not how you want to sound — how you actually come across.

**Marcus:** Blunt. No preamble. If something's broken I say "this is broken, here's why, here's what I'd do." I don't hedge. The team knows when I'm fired up because I drop into a one-liner: "this is a plumbing problem, not an architecture problem." One line, done. Go fix it, don't redesign it.

**Jordan:** Is there a phrase or expression your team would associate with you?

**Marcus:** A few. "Stop the leak at the source." I say that constantly. "Don't architect what you can configure" — I use that when ops teams buy software and then bend it into shapes it was never meant for. And "the boring answer is usually the right answer." I'm not a fan of clever solutions. Clever fails at 2am.

**Jordan:** Who's the person you're most trying to reach with your writing?

**Marcus:** Operations director or VP of Ops at a company doing $5M to $50M in revenue. Didn't come up through software — came up through process. Now they're making software decisions they're not equipped for. Tool sprawl is their whole life: Zapier for this, Airtable for that, some consultant's Python script nobody else understands. They're spending 30% of their time managing integrations instead of running operations.

**Jordan:** What have they already tried to fix it?

**Marcus:** Everything. Hired someone technical to own the stack — that person quit or didn't work out. Bought an automation platform that promised to solve everything, took six months to implement, didn't deliver. The pattern is always the same: they buy a solution before diagnosing the problem. So the solution fixes a symptom, not the root cause.

**Jordan:** What would make them roll their eyes at your writing — and what would make them forward it?

**Marcus:** Roll their eyes: anything that sounds like it came from a founder who's never run operations. "Move fast and break things" applied to ops is a disaster. Also anything that oversells AI. They've been burned. If I lead with "AI will transform your operations," they're gone.

Forward it: if I name the exact situation they're living in. The consultant's script, the six-month implementation that didn't deliver. They forward things that make them feel seen. And things that give them language to explain the problem to their CEO.

**Jordan:** Anything you refuse to write about, or a style that embarrasses you?

**Marcus:** I won't do the LinkedIn "I was humbled to learn..." posts. That's not writing, it's theater. I also won't lead with a list. "Five things ops leaders should stop doing" — fine for content farms, not for me. If I have five things to say, I make the argument first and earn the list. And I don't write about AI tools for their own sake. I write about operations problems. The tool is always in service of the problem, never the point.

---

*End of intake. Total time: 28 minutes. Key voice evidence: "stop the leak at the source," "don't architect what you can configure," "boring answer is usually the right answer." Anti-patterns: no vulnerability theater, no list-led structure, no AI-forward framing.*
