# Engine: Signature techniques

> **Status:** v1 cherry-picked from `{{VAULT_NAME}}/reference/voice-guide.md`. Curated to remove patterns that conflict with `voice.md`. Adds granular rhetorical moves beyond the 5 frameworks in `engine/frameworks.md`.

Techniques are *specific moves within frameworks*, not alternatives to them. Frameworks define the macro-structure of an argument. Techniques are concrete patterns the drafter reaches for inside that structure.

Use 1–3 techniques per piece, picked based on what the material naturally supports. Don't checklist them.

---

## Cross-audience techniques (work across both audiences)

### 1. Attributed personal moment (opener)

Open with a specific moment from a named person. Real conversation, real call, real story. Names the person.

**Pattern:** *"[Specific person] said [specific thing] that [specific reaction]."*

**Example:** *"My friend Eduardo told me his life had changed two weeks after I taught him to build with AI. The part you haven't heard: he's pre-selling tech stacks to competitors he hasn't built yet."*

**When to use:** Openers that earn trust through specificity. Works when the material includes a real conversation with a named person.

**Caution:** Don't formulaically use *"[Friend Name] shared an analogy that stopped me cold."* That's the AI-attributed-anecdote template. The technique is *named person + specific quote + specific moment*, not a recipe.

**Framework fit:** Default opener for **Garry-Tan-style**. Optional opener for **Contrast-driven** when the named person's story is the anti-pattern.

---

### 2. Direct-address tension

Put the reader on the spot with a hypothetical scenario plus *"you"* plus an uncomfortable observation. Forces self-recognition before offering the move.

**Pattern:** *"If [hypothetical], you'd probably [predictable response that exposes the gap]."*

**Example:** *"If I asked you what you were doing three days ago at this time, you'd probably say, 'Hmm, some sort of work, I think?'"*

**When to use:** In the problem section of any framework. Creates the *"I feel seen"* moment that earns trust for the move.

**Framework fit:** Pairs well with **PAS** (the agitate beat) and **BAB** (the Before).

---

### 3. Name the excuse, dismantle it

Quote what the audience tells themselves. Then reframe what's actually underneath.

**Pattern:** *"The most common excuse: '[reasonable-sounding justification].' It sounds [adjective]. Underneath it: [what's actually being avoided]."*

**Example:** *"The most common excuse I hear: 'I need to be accessible to my team.' It sounds responsible. Underneath it: there's a trust gap. There's an identity attachment to being needed."*

**When to use:** In the problem section, after stakes are clear. Works best with takes where {{VAULT_OWNER_SHORT_NAME}} is naming a belief they disagree with.

**Framework fit:** Strong with **Contrast-driven** and **PAS**.

---

### 4. Permission to let go

Acknowledge the emotional resistance to change — the part of the reader that doesn't want to give up control, delegate, or trust the system. Validate the feeling, then show what's on the other side.

**Pattern:** *"The biggest thing I had to give up to make this work was [specific control / habit / identity attachment]. Part of me still wants to [resist]. That's exactly why the system exists."*

**Example:** *"The biggest thing I had to give up was control. Part of me still wants to override the calendar every time. That's exactly why the system exists."*

**When to use:** After presenting the move. Readers rarely get permission to admit they're struggling with letting go — give it explicitly.

**Framework fit:** Closing pattern for **BAB** (the Bridge becomes a permission gift) or section-break pullback in **Garry-Tan-style**. Primarily audience-1-leaning, but the audience-2 equivalent is technique #10 below.

---

### 5. Big But + deep cut (mid-piece)

Build the apparent picture up. Then reverse it with *"but here's what nobody is saying"* logic. Pair the reversal with a **deep cut** — a specific, operational detail the reader couldn't have pulled from a Google search or AI summary. The combination is what creates the *I had no idea* moment that earns the rest of the piece.

- **Big But:** the structural reversal. *"This is impressive. The numbers check out. The marketing is gorgeous. But here's what nobody is saying…"*
- **Deep cut:** the substance the reversal lands on. The 20,000-line CLAUDE.md. The EA correcting line by line. The $150K stack the founder was spending on convenience. Inside-the-room specifics.

**Pattern:** *"[Two or three sentences of build-up. Establish the apparent picture.] But [specific reversal grounded in a deep cut]."*

**Example:** *"The Best Self brand is everywhere. Polished podcast, flagship product over a million units sold, full content engine. But Cathryn runs the whole operation with a four-person team and defends her calendar like a vault. The leverage isn't in the brand. It's in what she refuses to add."*

**When to use:** Mid-piece in any framework, especially after the opener has set the scene. The reversal earns the next section.

**Caution:** A Big But without a deep cut is empty drama. The reversal must land on substance the reader didn't already have. *"But here's the truth…"* with no specific revelation following is on the kill list.

**Framework fit:** Strong in **Garry-Tan-style** (the pivot), **Contrast-driven** (the move beat), and **PAS** (the agitate-to-solve transition).

---

### 6. Implications zoom-out

Take a small specific observation. Trace the second- and third-order consequences until the stakes feel real. The technique is not exaggeration — it's following the actual chain of what a small thing means.

**Pattern:** *"[Specific observation]. Which means [first-order consequence]. Which means [second-order consequence the reader hasn't connected yet]."*

**Example:** *"Most founders treat their AI assistant like a tool. Which means they keep doing the thinking and use AI for the typing. Which means they're paying for a faster keyboard, not a thinking partner. Which means they'll never feel the leverage they're paying for."*

**When to use:** When the topic risks landing as small. The specific observation alone reads as *"so what?"* — the zoom-out makes it land. Especially useful when a tactical move has identity-level implications.

**Caution:** Stop at the second or third level. Zooming out too far hits *grandiose stakes inflation* (see `kill-list.md`) — a CRM tag becomes the future of civilization. Calibrate to actual stakes.

**Framework fit:** Pairs well with **Garry-Tan-style** philosophical close. Sharpens the *"what doing this means about who you're becoming"* beat.

---

### 7. Learn-with-me stance

Write as the operator who is still figuring it out, not the authority who has it solved. The reader follows along, not below. Authority still comes through receipts — but the *posture* is inquisitive, not declarative.

**Pattern:** *"I [discovered, found, was sent, ran into] [specific thing]. At first I [reaction the reader probably shares]. Then [the shift]."*

**Example:** *"A friend sent me a paper on memory architecture for AI agents. I almost ignored it. Most of those papers are dead in two months. I read it eventually. It changed how I think about every Claude.md file I'll ever write."*

**When to use:** Openers, especially for technical or research-heavy material. Lets the reader trust you because you're showing the real path to the insight, not handing them a finished verdict.

**Caution:** This is *not* false humility. Don't perform *"I'm just a guy figuring this out."* {{VAULT_OWNER_SHORT_NAME}} is the operator who built it, and that authority needs to come through. The stance is *inquisitive expert*, not *naive seeker*. False vulnerability is on the kill list.

**Framework fit:** Default opener for **Garry-Tan-style** when the material has a real discovery moment. Pairs naturally with technique #1 (attributed personal moment) when the discovery came through someone else.

## Audience-1-leaning techniques

### 8. Specific receipt with stakes

Concrete figures that make the abstract problem real and quantifiable. Not ROI framing — making the invisible visible.

**Pattern:** *"If your time is worth [$X/hour] in your highest-value activities, and you're spending [Y%] of your time on things someone else could do, that's [$Z/hour] being wasted."*

**Example:** *"$150K a year in software subscriptions. Half of those tools we could rebuild ourselves in a few days each. That's the cost of buying convenience instead of building leverage."*

**When to use:** Sparingly. One per piece max. Best in the problem section to sharpen stakes. Must feel earned, not manufactured.

**Framework fit:** Sharpens the agitate beat in **PAS** or the Before in **BAB**.

**Cross-reference:** Overlaps with the receipts pattern in `voice.md`. Treat this as the structured form of the same instinct.

## Audience-2-leaning techniques

### 9. Urgency wake-up call (opener)

Open with a direct, urgent statement about the audience-2 role being at risk or shifting. No soft warm-up. The first line creates stakes.

**Pattern:** *"[Stark observation about the role, career, or future]."*

**Example:** *"The traditional EA role is becoming obsolete. The strategic-partner EA who builds with AI is becoming the only EA worth hiring."*

**When to use:** Opening section of audience-2 newsletters. Sets the tone — this isn't casual reading.

**Caution:** Vary the wake-up phrase. *"Let's not sugarcoat this"* used every issue becomes formulaic. Rotate the framing.

**Framework fit:** Opener for **Contrast-driven** or **PAS** in audience-2 content.

---

### 10. "You see it before they do"

Position the audience-2 reader as the one with visibility the audience-1 reader doesn't have. Validates their strategic awareness while naming the frustration of not acting on it.

**Pattern:** *"You see [specific signal] before they do. You're in the [specific channel]. You notice when [specific shift]."*

**Example:** *"You see it before they do. You're in the inbox. You're in the calendar. You notice when the energy shifts. You know something needs to change. And you say nothing."*

**When to use:** Early in audience-2 newsletters — opening hook or bridge to story. Creates the *"I feel seen"* moment.

**Framework fit:** Strong with **Contrast-driven** as the setup for *"and yet you stay silent — here's why that's the wrong move."*

---

### 11. The lane expansion

Name the fear audience-2 readers have about overstepping. Then reframe it as the job. Give explicit permission to step beyond traditional boundaries.

**Pattern:** *"You don't say it because [fear, training, past experience]. But [reframe — that's not overstepping, that's the job now]."*

**Example:** *"Because you're not sure it's your place. Because you've been trained to stay in your lane. That's not overstepping. That's an EA protecting what matters most."*

**When to use:** After establishing the problem. Works when the move requires readers to claim more ownership than they're used to.

**Framework fit:** The Solve beat of **PAS** or the Bridge of **BAB** in audience-2 content.

---

### 12. Script drop (Audience-2-only)

Exact, copy-paste language audience-2 readers can use with audience-1. Removes the *"I don't know how to bring this up"* barrier.

**Format:** Always blockquote + italics, with `[Name]` placeholder.

**Example:**
> *"Hey [Name], I've been thinking about how we manage your calendar. I'd like to try something for the next two weeks…"*

**When to use:** Throughout audience-2 newsletters, not limited to a specific section. Whenever a tactical move would benefit from verbatim language.

**Framework fit:** Independent of framework. Drop in wherever a tactical move would benefit from verbatim language.

**Limit:** Max 2 scripts per newsletter. More than that and the piece becomes a script library instead of an essay.

---

### 13. Career stakes reframe

Connect the specific topic back to the reader's career trajectory. Reframe tactical advice as a career-defining move.

**Pattern:** *"The [audience-2 readers] who [master this shift, build this skill, make this move] will [career outcome]. Others will [stay where they are]."*

**Example:** *"The EAs who master this shift will lead their organizations while others are still managing inboxes."*

**When to use:** Career reality section or close. One strong career-stakes statement per newsletter — don't repeat.

**Framework fit:** Closing pattern in **Contrast-driven** or **Garry-Tan-style**, audience-2-specific.

## Closing patterns (both audiences)

### 14. The honest note (close)

Direct, personal, raw closing that drops the teaching framework entirely. Speaks from the heart about what the reader can become. The mentor mask comes off.

**Pattern:** Short, declarative. No frameworks. Direct address.

**Example:** *"This is the most pivotal year for your career so far. I want you on the winning side of this."*

**When to use:** Final section of long-form pieces (newsletters, X articles). The emotional payoff after the tactical middle.

**Caution:** Don't title this section *"An Honest Note"* every issue. The technique is the *move*, not the heading. Vary the framing — sometimes a labeled section, sometimes the closing paragraph, sometimes a P.S. Repeating the heading turns it into template.

**Format limit:** When labeled as a section, two paragraphs max.

**Framework fit:** Default close for **Garry-Tan-style** when the philosophical pullback wants a more vulnerable register than the standard reframe.

## How techniques relate to frameworks

Frameworks define the *structure* of the argument. Techniques are the *moves* inside that structure. Every framework supports multiple techniques. Pick techniques based on:

1. What the material naturally supports — named people invite #1, deep-cut specifics invite #5, dollar figures invite #8, audience-2-specific tension invites #10–#13.
2. Which audience you're writing for — audience-2-specific techniques only fire on audience-2 content; #4 primarily on audience-1 content.
3. Which beat of the framework you're at — openers (#1, #2, #7, #9, #10), problem (#3), mid-piece pivot (#5), solve (#4, #11), close (#6, #14), independent (#8, #12, #13).

When in doubt: fewer techniques used well beats more techniques crammed in.
