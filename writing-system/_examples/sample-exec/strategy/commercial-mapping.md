# Content Strategy — Commercial Mapping

> **Status:** Populated for Sarah Chen / Gridline. Maps each domain to Gridline's commercial offer and clarifies which domain warrants direct CTAs vs. soft pointers vs. brand-only. No domain is pure thought leadership — all three have a commercial path, but the directness gradient runs D2 → D1 → D3.

This file maps each content domain to Gridline's commercial offer so the writing-system knows when to reach for direct CTAs vs. soft pointers vs. brand-building-only.

---

## How direct vs. soft CTAs work per domain

**D2 — Direct CTA native.** The receipt is Gridline. D2 pieces are the content form of Gridline's sales motion — they establish the problem (AI tooling that doesn't compound), articulate the category (AI-native infrastructure), and earn the right to close with a demo link or trial CTA. *"If your team is trying to get this right, Gridline is what we built to address it."* Direct without being salesy, because the piece IS the demonstration.

**D1 — Soft pointer, occasional direct CTA.** D1 pieces lead with the engineering problem (velocity leaks, handoff failures, debt budget design). The Gridline mention, if it appears, is subordinate: *"The infrastructure layer is one place this shows up — it's part of why we built Gridline."* The CTA is more often newsletter subscription or a link to a longer Gridline resource. Don't open a D1 piece with a product mention.

**D3 — Brand-building, no direct product CTA.** D3 pieces are personal-craft essays. Inserting a Gridline CTA into a piece about Sarah's own technical identity as a founder reads as a misfire. The commercial return on D3 is long-tail: readers who follow Sarah for D3 content trust her judgment, and that trust is what eventually converts them into Gridline customers. The CTA — if any — is newsletter subscription, not a demo link.

## Cross-cutting notes

- **No domain is pure thought leadership.** D3 comes closest, but the commercial return is real — it's just indirect and long-tail. Every domain earns something.
- **The receipt is Gridline.** Sarah's voice uses *"the receipt is X"* framing. In D2 pieces especially, Gridline is the receipt — the specific shipped artifact that validates the claim. This isn't a sales technique; it's how the voice works. The product is proof, not pitch.
- **D1 and D2 are editorially distinct but commercially adjacent.** A reader who converts from a D1 piece and one who converts from a D2 piece are probably the same person at different stages of awareness. D1 names the pain; D2 names the solution category; Gridline sits at the end of D2's content path.
- **D3's commercial value is difficult to measure short-term.** Don't cut D3 because it doesn't generate demo requests. Its job is to build the audience that generates D1 and D2 readers. A founder who follows Sarah for D3 brings peers along — and those peers are D1/D2 readers.
