# Content Strategy — Domains

> **Status:** Populated for Sarah Chen / Gridline. Three domains identified, defined, and filter-tested against Sarah's material well, audience, and Gridline's commercial positioning. See [[pillars]] for sub-topics per domain.

A **domain** is a stable, recurring content stream — a content area Sarah runs over time. Each domain has a clear identity, a target audience, and an editorial purpose distinct from other domains. Domains sit above pillars (sub-topics) and below the overall content strategy.

Sarah runs **three content domains.** Two target the primary audience (SaaS founders / founder-engineers); one carries dual-audience weight, serving both that audience and the IC-to-leadership transitioner.

---

## D1 — Engineering Velocity at Scale

**Slug:** `engineering-velocity`

**Definition.** How small/medium engineering teams ship faster as they grow without losing depth or compounding tech debt.

**Identity.** The practical craft of building engineering teams that stay fast at 10, 25, and 50 engineers. Not productivity hacks — infrastructure design decisions. Sarah's domain here is structural: how do you architect the team's process, handoff patterns, and debt budget so velocity is a property of the system, not a function of heroics?

**Why this domain (Sarah/Gridline rationale).** This is Gridline's origin story. Sarah built Gridline because she watched velocity degrade at scale in her own engineering orgs. Every D1 piece is pulling from real decisions she's made, seen fail, or watched compound. The receipt-is-X framing lives most naturally here. Commercially, D1 is the top of Gridline's funnel — the reader who nods through a D1 piece is exactly who Gridline serves.

**Audience served.** SaaS founders primary (they own the engineering function and feel the velocity drag directly). IC-to-leadership transitioners secondary (engineering managers crossing into leadership are the ones who discover these problems firsthand at the team level).

**Filter check.**
- Material: Strong. Sarah has real decisions across handoff design, tech-debt budgeting, and velocity degradation — builder-as-witness receipts available.
- Specific audience: Yes — founders running engineering functions.
- Commercial connection: Top-of-funnel for Gridline. D1 content earns trust with the people Gridline converts.

## D2 — AI-Native Infrastructure

**Slug:** `ai-native-infra`

**Definition.** Building product engineering systems where AI tooling compounds (each tool's output feeds the next), not where AI is bolted on as a feature.

**Identity.** The discipline of actually integrating AI into engineering infrastructure — not wrapping a product feature with a model, but redesigning the engineering system so AI is load-bearing. How to evaluate tools by whether they compound or just add surface area. Why agent harness design is now a core infrastructure decision. When to build vs. buy in the AI layer. Sarah's domain here is architectural: most teams bolt on AI; she writes about building it in.

**Why this domain (Sarah/Gridline rationale).** This *is* Gridline's positioning. AI-native infrastructure is the category Sarah is building the company inside. D2 content isn't warming up readers for Gridline — it IS the Gridline thesis, made public. The pieces that convert directly sit here. Gridline's ICP is a team trying to figure out exactly what Sarah writes about in D2.

**Audience served.** SaaS founders primary (infrastructure decisions belong to founders and their head of engineering). IC-to-leadership transitioners light secondary (staff engineers crossing into tech lead or head-of-eng territory start facing these decisions).

**Filter check.**
- Material: Strong. Gridline's product is the material. Every architectural decision Sarah has made building the company is a potential D2 receipt.
- Specific audience: Yes — founders making infrastructure calls.
- Commercial connection: Direct. D2 is where Gridline converts. CTAs to demo, trial, or Gridline docs sit naturally here.

## D3 — Founder-as-Builder

**Slug:** `founder-as-builder`

**Definition.** The IC-to-founder transition: keeping technical depth while running a company, deciding when to abstract vs. ship-direct.

**Identity.** The personal craft of being a founder who used to ship things and still wants to. When does staying technical compound, and when does it become a delegation failure? How do you hire for technical taste when you're no longer writing the code? How do you know when your instincts are still reliable? Sarah's domain here is confessional and specific — the moments she got this right, the moments she got it wrong, the heuristics she's actually running on.

**Why this domain (Sarah/Gridline rationale).** This is Sarah's hardest-to-replicate authority. Anyone can write about engineering velocity; only someone who has made the IC-to-founder crossing and stayed partially technical can write this from the inside. D3 builds personal brand without being personal-life content. It also serves the IC-to-leadership audience directly — the engineering manager who is 18 months from making this same crossing reads D3 as a map, not a memoir.

**Audience served.** SaaS founders primary (specifically those who came up through engineering and are renegotiating their technical identity). IC-to-leadership transitioners equally primary — this is the cross-audience domain. The engineering manager who picks up this newsletter because of D1 and D2 stays for D3.

**Filter check.**
- Material: Strong. Sarah's own crossing from IC to Gridline CEO is the primary anchor. Specific decisions — when she last wrote production code, when she stopped, when she abstracted — are the receipt material.
- Specific audience: Yes, and uniquely dual — both audiences are real readers of D3.
- Commercial connection: Indirect. D3 builds personal brand and trust. The conversion path is long — someone who follows Sarah for D3 content will eventually trust Gridline enough to take a demo.

## Boundary notes

- **D1 vs D2.** Both involve how engineering teams operate. D1 is about team structure, process, and debt — it could be written without ever mentioning AI. D2 is specifically about AI tooling integration at the infrastructure level. A piece on team handoffs is D1. A piece on where AI-generated code fits into the handoff system is D2.
- **D2 vs D3.** Both can involve AI. D2 asks *"how does the engineering system incorporate AI?"* D3 asks *"how does the founder who still thinks in code navigate an AI-transformed landscape?"* D2 is about the org's infrastructure; D3 is about Sarah's personal technical identity.
- **D1 and D3 overlap on hiring.** A D1 piece about hiring for engineering quality sits in D1. A D3 piece about how Sarah detects technical taste in a candidate when she's no longer writing production code sits in D3. Test: is the subject the engineering system (D1) or Sarah's evolving instincts as a founder (D3)?
